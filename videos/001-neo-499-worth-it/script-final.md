I have wanted a robot in my house since I was about eight years old. And this year, for the first time in human history, that's an actual product. ### SECTION 1 — WHAT YOU'RE ACTUALLY BUYING (0:50 – 4:30)

**[B-ROLL: 1X NEO product shots, size comparison graphics]**

The robot is called NEO, made by 1X Technologies — a Norwegian-American company that OpenAI invested in early, back in twenty twenty-three, and that's now reportedly raising a billion dollars at a ten-billion-dollar valuation. Serious people, serious money. And the first thing you need to know is the price structure, because "the $499 robot" is only half the story.

There are two ways in. Buy it outright: twenty thousand dollars. Or subscribe: four ninety-nine a month, with a reported six-month minimum. Either way, you put down a two-hundred-dollar deposit — fully refundable — and here's fine-print item number one: you're not buying a robot today.

Because despite all of the videos and headlines, there have been zero customer deliveries as I record this, in July of twenty twenty-six. Not one. What you're buying is a place in line. The first production run — about ten thousand robots — sold out in five days last October, and deliveries are expected later this year. Every jaw-dropping clip you've seen is a controlled demonstration — a company demo, or a journalist demo. Hold that thought. It matters for everything that follows.

A quick word on who's building it, because it tells you how real this is. 1X has a fifty-eight-thousand-square-foot factory in Hayward, California, tooled for about ten thousand robots a year, with a stated plan to grow that tenfold by the end of next year. This is not two guys and a demo video. The robots are coming. The only question — the whole question — is whether you should be at the front of that line.

Now, the machine itself — and honestly, the engineering is beautiful. NEO is five foot six and weighs sixty-six pounds, which is a real design decision: a three-hundred-pound industrial robot falling over in your kitchen is a lawsuit; a sixty-six-pound one is a mess. It's tendon-driven instead of geared, which is why it moves soft and quiet instead of like a forklift — twenty-two decibels, quieter than your refrigerator. It wears an actual knit nylon suit, which sounds like a gimmick until you realize it's padding, because this thing lives where you live. Battery is about four hours of runtime on a charge. Real thought went into this machine.

What does it do? The company's list: opens doors, works light switches, fetches things, tidies rooms, wipes surfaces, waters plants, answers questions — there's a language model in there. But the most honest picture we have is the Wall Street Journal's demo, where NEO took about five minutes to open a dishwasher and put three things inside — and every one of those actions was controlled by a human operator.

And look — when the first cars came out, they lost races to horses. That's not an insult. That's just where we are on the curve. But that phrase — "controlled by a human operator" — is fine-print item number two, and it's the single most important thing in this video.

### SECTION 2 — THE PERSON INSIDE THE ROBOT (4:30 – 8:30)

**[B-ROLL: teleoperation footage — operator with VR headset controlling robot]**

Here's how NEO handles a task its AI can't do yet: a trained 1X operator, wearing a VR headset in an office, sees through your robot's cameras and takes over its hands. 1X calls it Expert Mode.

The product in the ad is an autonomous robot. The product in your house is, some of the time, a human presence wearing a robot body.

Now — I find this genuinely fascinating rather than sinister, and I'll tell you why. But first, the facts, because they're better than the internet rumor version: nothing connects without you. You schedule Expert sessions through the app and approve each one. You can mark rooms as no-go zones the robot can't enter even under teleoperation. Faces are blurred for the operator by default, audio can be masked, there's a data-sharing opt-out, and the operators are background-checked and don't know whose home they're in. This is not a hidden-camera scandal. It's disclosed. It's actually a clever way to ship a robot before the AI is finished.

And I'll tell you where I'm coming from on this. I work in cybersecurity, and it has fundamentally changed how I evaluate new technology. Most people look at a humanoid robot and immediately ask what chores it can do. I ask a different set of questions. Who has access to the cameras? What data leaves the home? How are remote sessions authenticated? What happens when the robot encounters something it can't understand? Those aren't questions designed to create fear — they're the same questions I ask about every connected device, because history has shown that convenience almost always arrives before the security conversation catches up.

So let me answer my own questions, based on what's public. Access is session-based and you approve each one — that's genuinely better than most security cameras, which are just… on. Data leaves the home as training footage unless you opt out, so the honest framing is that the default is sharing. Authentication and session security — 1X says the right things, but no one outside the company has kicked the tires yet, because, again, nobody has one. That's not a red flag. It's just the actual state of play: decent design on paper, unproven in the wild. Every connected product you own started exactly here.

But here's what Expert Mode really tells you about this product — and this is the reframe that changes the whole buying decision:

You're not buying a robot. You're buying a seat in the training fleet. Every task an operator performs through your NEO becomes training data that teaches the AI to do it on its own. The whole business model is that thousands of homes generate the data that makes version three actually good. Early buyers aren't just customers — they're the teachers.

Which is noble! That's exactly how self-driving cars got trained. It's just worth knowing, before you spend twenty grand, whether you're paying to be the teacher — or waiting to hire the graduate.

If a scheduled human presence in your home is a hard no for you, at any price, this product is not for you yet, and that's a completely reasonable place to land. For everyone else, it's a math question.

### SECTION 3 — THE HONEST MATH (8:30 – 13:00)

**[GRAPHIC: cost comparison table — build line by line as narrated (slides 01–03)]**

Let's price out what NEO actually replaces, because "it does chores" is not a number.

Generous scenario: NEO reliably saves you seven hours of housework a week — tidying, laundry, resetting rooms. Hired house cleaning in the US runs about thirty to fifty dollars an hour, so seven hours is real money IF you were actually paying someone. Most people aren't. Most people just… do it, or don't.

So compare three real options.

**Option one: the boring stack.** A flagship robot vacuum-mop — list price is sixteen, seventeen hundred these days, but they're a thousand dollars on sale constantly. Add a human cleaner twice a month at about a hundred and fifty per visit. First year, all in: call it forty-six hundred to five thousand dollars. And here's the thing — this stack works TODAY. The vacuum doesn't fall over. The cleaner cleans bathrooms, which NEO cannot do.

**Option two: NEO on subscription.** Six thousand a year. It covers the tidying-and-laundry lane — slowly, and partly via Expert Mode — but you likely still want the vacuum, and you still need the human for deep cleaning. So realistically it's six thousand ON TOP of some of option one, not instead of it.

And let me make that concrete, because "it covers the tidying lane" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Picture an actual week with this thing, based on what it can do today. Monday afternoon, it resets the living room — picking things up off the floor, straightening, slow and steady, on a four-hour battery. Somewhere in there it hits something it can't figure out — a latch, a weird cabinet, a pile it doesn't understand — and now you're opening the app to schedule an Expert session, which means picking a time window and approving a human operator to steer it through. The laundry gets loaded. Eventually. The bathroom stays exactly as dirty as it was, because that's not on the menu. None of this is a knock — version one of everything looks like this. But when the price is six thousand dollars a year, "what does a normal Tuesday actually look like" is the question that matters, and the honest answer is: helpful, slow, and supervised.

**Option three: NEO purchased outright.** Twenty thousand up front, on a version-one product, before a single customer review exists — for a machine whose successor, by the company's own roadmap and everyone else's, will be dramatically better within two to three years. Twenty grand on gen one is what early adopters paid for the first plasma TVs. You know how that went.

On pure dollars, the boring stack wins. It's not close. Anyone who tells you NEO "pays for itself" in saved time is doing influencer math.

BUT — and this is where I'll genuinely defend the robot — pure dollars is the wrong frame for two groups of people. If you have mobility limitations, the comparison isn't "cheaper than a cleaner," it's "can I stay independent in my own home" — and assisted living now runs about seventy-five thousand dollars a year at the national median. Against that, six grand a year for a capability that improves every month is a completely different conversation.

I want to sit on that for one more second, because it's the least talked-about and most important use case in this entire category. For someone who needs help reaching things, carrying things, or just having another set of hands in the house, "slow but tireless" isn't a bug — it's the entire point. The robot doesn't get impatient. It doesn't watch the clock. And the subscription means a family isn't gambling twenty thousand dollars on version-one hardware — they're renting help that gets better every month, and they can stop any time the math stops working. If that's your situation, ignore my verdict and run your own numbers. And if you're a developer or a business building on robotics, NEO is the cheapest general-purpose home humanoid with real support you can put in a real house — Unitree's G1 is thirteen and a half thousand, but that's a research platform, not a housemate.

For everyone else? The math says wait. And "wait" has its own number, so let's put a number on it.

### SECTION 4 — THE WAIT MATH (13:00 – 17:30)

**[B-ROLL: factory footage — Tesla Optimus line, Figure BotQ, Unitree]**

Here's what's happening on the supply side while you're deciding — and honestly, this is the part that makes me optimistic.

Twenty twenty-six is the first year of genuine humanoid mass production. Tesla built an Optimus line designed for up to a million units a year — though, being honest about Tesla timelines, production is only just starting and they've missed every robot target they've set so far. Figure built a factory called BotQ tooled for about twelve thousand humanoids a year. And China is the real story: Unitree's G1 is down from sixteen thousand to thirteen and a half. Their newer R1 is under five thousand dollars. And at the bottom of the market there's a fourteen-hundred-dollar humanoid called the Bumi — it's a two-foot-tall classroom toy, not a housemaid, but a fourteen-hundred-dollar humanoid existing at all tells you where the cost curve is pointed.

It's worth thirty seconds on what each of those actually is, because the names get thrown around like they're interchangeable, and they're not. Optimus is the famous one, and it's still basically a promise — a few hundred pilot units working inside Tesla's own buildings, nothing you can buy, and a reveal date that keeps sliding. Figure is the serious industrial play — their third-generation robot is coming off that BotQ line at a claimed rate of one an hour, but those are headed to warehouses and partner companies, not kitchens. Unitree is the one you can actually order today — the G1 is a genuinely capable machine, but out of the box it's a research platform for robotics labs, not a housemate. Nobody's G1 is folding their laundry. And the Bumi dances for schoolchildren. So here's the honest snapshot of twenty twenty-six: NEO is, right now, the only general-purpose humanoid you can order for your actual house. That's a real first-mover position — and it's also exactly why they can charge twenty thousand dollars for it.

The prices back this up. Goldman Sachs watched humanoid build costs drop about forty percent in a single year — they'd expected fifteen to twenty. Bank of America's forecast has costs halving by twenty thirty. That's the same curve that ran over drones and robot vacuums — the capability that cost a thousand dollars in twenty fifteen sells for a couple hundred now.

If you want to know how this movie goes, look at drones. In twenty twelve, putting a good camera in the air meant a five-figure professional rig and a trained pilot. Then DJI industrialized it, and within a few years a better shot cost under a thousand dollars and showed up at every wedding in America. Robot vacuums ran the same arc — the Roomba that cost nine hundred dollars in twenty fifteen has descendants at a fifth the price that navigate better than it ever did. Humanoids are earlier on that curve, and they're far more complicated machines. But the physics of the curve don't care. Scale plus competition equals collapsing prices. The only argument is about the date.

One honest wrinkle, because I promised you fine print. There's a bill in Congress right now — the GUARD Act — and it proposes restricting Chinese-built humanoid robots from the US market, pending national security review. So instead of assuming Americans will immediately benefit from the aggressive pricing happening overseas, the more realistic conclusion is that the price war may take considerably longer to reach US consumers. If that passes, robots like NEO keep their pricing power longer. Doesn't change the direction. Changes the date.

So run the wait scenario. You wait until twenty twenty-eight. You've spent zero dollars. The robot you can buy then is a generation better — trained, remember, on the data from today's Expert Mode sessions — and meaningfully cheaper. The buyer who subscribed in twenty twenty-six spent roughly fourteen thousand dollars getting there, and owns nothing.

The strongest case FOR buying now is honestly not about chores at all. It's that you want to be early. Some people bought an Apple II in nineteen seventy-eight and it bent the whole direction of their life — not because it was useful, it barely was — but because they got three years of fluency before everyone else. If twenty thousand dollars is money you can genuinely afford to burn on being three years early to the defining technology of the next decade, that's a real reason. It's just not the reason in the ad.

For the other ninety-five percent of us: the robot you actually want is the one this robot's cameras are currently training.

### SECTION 5 — THE VERDICT (17:30 – 20:30)

**[GRAPHIC: decision tree — build as narrated (slides 04–05)]**

So here's the whole video in four sentences.

If you're buying NEO to save money on chores — don't. The boring stack of a robot vacuum plus occasional human help costs less, works better, and works today.

If you have accessibility needs, or you're building on robotics professionally — the math genuinely changes, and NEO is worth a serious look. For accessibility in particular, the subscription structure is actually an advantage: you're renting a capability that improves every month, with none of the version-one depreciation risk.

If you want it because it's the future and you can afford to be early — subscribe, don't buy. Five hundred a month with a reported six-month minimum is a fair price for a front-row seat you can walk away from. Twenty thousand locked into gen-one hardware, sight unseen, before the first customer review exists — is not.

And if you're everyone else: wait, and I mean that as a genuine promise, not a brush-off. Set the trigger with me: the day a humanoid can clean a bathroom unattended AND costs under ten grand — that's the buy signal. My honest guess is twenty twenty-eight, maybe twenty twenty-nine. Until then, other people's subscriptions are training your future robot — for free, from your point of view. That's the best deal in tech right now.

What would change my mind? Two things, and I'll cover both on this channel the moment they happen: full-task autonomy without Expert Mode — and the price war, if Chinese home humanoids ever clear the wall Congress is building. Either one flips this verdict. That's the next video.
