**[B-ROLL: teleoperation footage — operator with VR headset controlling robot]**

Here's how NEO handles a task its AI can't do yet: a trained 1X operator, wearing a VR headset in an office, sees through your robot's cameras and takes over its hands. 1X calls it **Expert Mode**. `[VERIFIED: "Expert Mode," remote 1X operator pilots via VR; scheduled through the app — 1x.tech/neo; Humanoids Daily, Oct 2025]`

The product in the ad is an autonomous robot. The product in your house is, some of the time, a human presence wearing a robot body.

Now — I find this genuinely fascinating rather than sinister, and I'll tell you why. But first, the facts, because they're better than the internet rumor version: nothing connects without you. You schedule Expert sessions through the app and approve each one. You can mark rooms as no-go zones the robot can't enter even under teleoperation. Faces are blurred for the operator by default, audio can be masked, there's a data-sharing opt-out, and the operators are background-checked and don't know whose home they're in. `[VERIFIED: session approval/scheduling, no-go zones, default face blurring, audio masking, data opt-out, background-checked operators — Humanoids Daily; Engadget Oct 2025; captaincompliance.com]` This is not a hidden-camera scandal. It's disclosed. It's actually a clever way to ship a robot before the AI is finished.

But it tells you what this product really is right now — and this is the reframe that changes the whole buying decision:

**You're not buying a robot. You're buying a seat in the training fleet.** Every task an operator performs through your NEO becomes training data that teaches the AI to do it on its own. The whole business model is that thousands of homes generate the data that makes version three actually good. Early buyers aren't just customers — they're the teachers.

Which is noble! That's exactly how self-driving cars got trained. It's just worth knowing, before you spend twenty grand, whether you're paying to be the teacher — or waiting to hire the graduate.

If a scheduled human presence in your home is a hard no for you, at any price, this product is not for you yet, and that's a completely reasonable place to land. For everyone else, it's a math question.

### SECTION 3 — THE HONEST MATH (8:30 – 13:00)

**[GRAPHIC: cost comparison table — build it line by line as narrated (slides 01–03)]**

Let's price out what NEO actually replaces, because "it does chores" is not a number.

Generous scenario: NEO reliably saves you seven hours of housework a week — tidying, laundry, resetting rooms. Hired house cleaning in the US runs about thirty to fifty dollars an hour `[VERIFIED: Angi $25–50/hr; HomeAdvisor avg ~$50/hr for companies — homeadvisor.com, updated 2026-06-19]`, so seven hours is real money IF you were actually paying someone. Most people aren't. Most people just… do it, or don't.

So compare three real options.

**Option one: the boring stack.** A flagship robot vacuum-mop — list price is sixteen, seventeen hundred these days, but they're a thousand dollars on sale constantly `[VERIFIED: Roborock/Dreame flagships $1,599–1,799 list, multiple current models $999 on sale — us.roborock.com fetched 2026-07-09; vacuumwars.com 2026]`. Add a human cleaner twice a month at about a hundred and fifty per visit `[VERIFIED: $100–250/visit typical, Angi avg $176 — homeadvisor.com 2026-06-19]`. First year, all in: call it forty-six hundred to five thousand dollars. And here's the thing — this stack works TODAY. The vacuum doesn't fall over. The cleaner cleans bathrooms, which NEO cannot do. `[AFFILIATE: robot vacuum link goes here — this is the video's conversion point. [BRIAN: pick model + program]]`

**Option two: NEO on subscription.** Six thousand a year. It covers the tidying-and-laundry lane — slowly, and partly via Expert Mode — but you likely still want the vacuum, and you still need the human for deep cleaning. So realistically it's six thousand ON TOP of some of option one, not instead of it.

**Option three: NEO purchased outright.** Twenty thousand up front, on a version-one product, before a single customer review exists — for a machine whose successor, by the company's own roadmap and everyone else's, will be dramatically better within two to three years. Twenty grand on gen one is what early adopters paid for the first plasma TVs. You know how that went.

On pure dollars, the boring stack wins. It's not close. Anyone who tells you NEO "pays for itself" in saved time is doing influencer math.

BUT — and this is where I'll genuinely defend the robot — pure dollars is the wrong frame for two groups of people. If you have mobility limitations, the comparison isn't "cheaper than a cleaner," it's "can I stay independent in my own home" — and assisted living now runs about seventy-five thousand dollars a year at the national median. `[VERIFIED: $74,400/yr ($6,200/mo) median — CareScout/Genworth 2025 Cost of Care Survey, released 2026-03-02]` Against that, six grand a year for a capability that improves every month is a completely different conversation. And if you're a developer or a business building on robotics, NEO is the cheapest general-purpose home humanoid with real support you can put in a real house — Unitree's G1 is thirteen and a half thousand, but that's a research platform, not a housemate. `[VERIFIED: G1 $13,500 base on official Unitree store, fetched 2026-07-09 — down from $16K at launch]`

For everyone else? The math says wait. And "wait" has its own number, so let's put a number on it.

So run the wait scenario. You wait until twenty twenty-eight. You've spent zero dollars. The robot you can buy then is a generation better — trained, remember, on the data from today's Expert Mode sessions — and meaningfully cheaper. The buyer who subscribed in twenty twenty-six spent roughly fourteen thousand dollars getting there, and owns nothing.

The strongest case FOR buying now is honestly not about chores at all. It's that you want to be early. Some people bought an Apple II in nineteen seventy-eight and it bent the whole direction of their life — not because it was useful, it barely was — but because they got three years of fluency before everyone else. If twenty thousand dollars is money you can genuinely afford to burn on being three years early to the defining technology of the next decade, that's a real reason. It's just not the reason in the ad.

For the other ninety-five percent of us: the robot you actually want is the one this robot's cameras are currently training.

### SECTION 5 — THE VERDICT (17:30 – 20:30)

**[GRAPHIC: decision tree — build as narrated (slides 04–05)]**

So here's the whole video in four sentences.

If you're buying NEO to save money on chores — don't. The boring stack of a robot vacuum plus occasional human help costs less, works better, and works today.

If you have accessibility needs, or you're building on robotics professionally — the math genuinely changes, and NEO is worth a serious look. For accessibility in particular, the subscription structure is actually an advantage: you're renting a capability that improves every month, with none of the version-one depreciation risk.

If you want it because it's the future and you can afford to be early — subscribe, don't buy. Five hundred a month with a reported six-month minimum is a fair price for a front-row seat you can walk away from. `[BRIAN: cancellation terms beyond the 6-month report are UNVERIFIED — soften or confirm]` Twenty thousand locked into gen-one hardware, sight unseen, before the first customer review exists — is not.

And if you're everyone else: wait, and I mean that as a genuine promise, not a brush-off. Set the trigger with me: **the day a humanoid can clean a bathroom unattended AND costs under ten grand — that's the buy signal.** My honest guess is twenty twenty-eight, maybe twenty twenty-nine. Until then, other people's subscriptions are training your future robot — for free, from your point of view. That's the best deal in tech right now.

What would change my mind? Two things, and I'll cover both on this channel the moment they happen: full-task autonomy without Expert Mode — and the price war, if Chinese home humanoids ever clear the wall Congress is building. Either one flips this verdict. That's the next video.
