# Production Update — Operation SIGNAL #001 (Brian, 2026-07-09)

The fact-checking process ended up changing more than I expected, but in a good way. Instead of weakening the story, it made it significantly more accurate and, honestly, more interesting.

The biggest correction was realizing that **nobody actually owns a NEO yet**. Despite all of the videos and headlines, there have been **zero customer deliveries** as of today. What you're buying is a place in line. The preorder requires a **$200 refundable deposit**, the first production run of 10,000 units reportedly sold out in just five days, and customer deliveries are expected later in 2026. That also meant removing every reference to "owner reviews." Those don't exist yet. What we do have are controlled demonstrations, including the Wall Street Journal's teleoperated demo. Opening the video with the fact that you're joining a waitlist instead of buying a finished product is a much stronger hook than pretending people are already living with these robots.

The second major correction involved the Chinese robotics market. My original script implied that the GUARD Act would clear Chinese humanoid robots for the U.S. market, but the reality is the opposite. The legislation proposes restricting Chinese-built humanoid robots pending national security review. That completely changes the takeaway. Instead of assuming Americans will immediately benefit from the aggressive pricing we've seen overseas, the more realistic conclusion is that the price war may take considerably longer to reach U.S. consumers. That also creates a natural setup for a future video focused entirely on robotics policy and regulation.

I also refreshed every major number in the script. NEO's published specifications now reflect its current weight and battery life. Competitor pricing has been updated to include the latest Unitree models. Assisted-living and professional cleaning costs were refreshed using current market data. More importantly, I removed vague analyst claims like "prices will drop 30–50% in a few years" and replaced them with projections from identifiable institutions such as Goldman Sachs and Bank of America. If someone asks where a number came from, I can point to an actual source instead of repeating something that's been copied across the internet.

Another area that needed clarification was teleoperation. 1X officially refers to this capability as **Expert Mode**, and it's an important distinction because the company isn't hiding the feature—they're documenting it. The current privacy controls have been verified against the latest public information, while the reported six-month subscription minimum remains clearly labeled as press-reported rather than something explicitly stated on the company's ordering page. I'd rather hedge a claim than overstate it.

## Where the project stands

Every major claim has been reviewed. The tone has been cleaned up. The facts have been verified. The script is now based on primary or clearly attributable sources instead of assumptions or recycled talking points.

## My Human Pass

This isn't just about changing a few words to satisfy an AI checklist. This is where the video actually becomes mine.

I'm going through the script line by line and rewriting sections in my own voice where it makes sense. More importantly, I'm adding perspectives that only come from my own background.

Working in cybersecurity has fundamentally changed how I evaluate new technology. Most people look at a humanoid robot and immediately ask what chores it can do. I ask a different set of questions. Who has access to the cameras? What data leaves the home? How are remote sessions authenticated? What happens if the robot encounters something it can't understand? Those aren't questions designed to create fear—they're the same questions I ask about every connected device because history has shown that convenience almost always arrives before the security conversation catches up.

That's the perspective I want this channel to bring. I'm fascinated by robotics. I genuinely believe humanoid robots will become part of everyday life. But I also think they're too important to cover like another gadget launch. My goal isn't to hype the technology or tear it down. It's to understand how it actually works, separate marketing from reality, and help people decide when these products are truly ready for their homes.
