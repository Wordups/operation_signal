### COLD OPEN (0:00 – 0:50)

**[B-ROLL: NEO promo footage — robot loading a dishwasher, folding laundry]**

Right now, you can go to a website, type in your credit card, and order a humanoid robot for your house. Five hundred dollars a month — about the price of a car payment — and a five-foot-six robot shows up at your door, walks into your kitchen, and starts doing your chores.

That's the pitch. And if you've seen the demo videos, it looks like the future finally arrived.

But I spent two weeks reading every spec sheet, every early-access review, every line of the terms of service — and there are three things buried in the fine print that completely change the math on this thing. One of them involves a stranger being able to see inside your house.

By the end of this video you'll know exactly what five hundred a month actually buys, what it can't do — and whether you should buy now, wait two years, or never. Let's start with what this thing actually is.

Now, the machine itself. ### SECTION 2 — THE PART THEY DON'T PUT IN THE AD (4:30 – 8:30)

**[B-ROLL: teleoperation footage — operator with VR headset controlling robot]**

Here's the single most important thing in this entire video.

A lot of what NEO does in your home — especially early on — is not done by artificial intelligence. It's done by a human being. A trained operator, sitting in an office, wearing a VR headset, seeing through your robot's cameras and controlling its hands. 1X calls this something like "expert mode" `[VERIFY: current branding + opt-in mechanics]` — when the robot hits a task its AI can't handle, a remote human can take over and do it through the robot.

Sit with that for a second. The product in the ad is an autonomous robot. The product in your house is, part of the time, a very expensive puppet with a stranger's eyes behind it.

Now — to be fair, and this matters — it's disclosed, it's scheduled, you approve when operators can connect, faces get blurred, there are no-go zones you can set `[VERIFY: current privacy controls, this list changes]`. This isn't a hidden camera scandal. But it changes what you're buying in two huge ways.

First: privacy. You are inviting a device into your home whose core feature — the reason it can handle tasks its AI can't — is a remote human presence. If that sentence made your skin crawl, this product is not for you yet, at any price.

Second, and this is the part almost nobody says out loud: **you are not buying a robot. You are buying a subscription to a training-data program.** Every task an operator performs through your NEO becomes training data that teaches the AI to do it autonomously. Early buyers aren't customers. They're the training fleet. 1X is fairly open about this if you read carefully — the whole business model is that thousands of homes generate the data that makes the robot actually good in three years.

That's not a scam. It's exactly how self-driving cars got trained. But it reframes the buying decision completely: the question isn't "is this robot worth $499 a month" — it's "do I want to pay $499 a month to help train version three, or wait and buy version three."

### SECTION 3 — THE HONEST MATH (8:30 – 13:00)

**[GRAPHIC: cost comparison table, build it line by line as narrated]**

Let's price out what NEO actually replaces, because "it does chores" is not a number.

Take a generous scenario: NEO reliably saves you seven hours of housework a week — tidying, laundry, resetting rooms. At the median US cost of house cleaning, call it thirty to forty dollars an hour `[VERIFY: current rate]`, seven hours is roughly two hundred fifty bucks a week IF you were actually paying someone. Most people aren't. Most people just... do it, or don't.

So compare three real options.

**Option one: the boring stack.** A flagship robot vacuum-mop, about a thousand dollars. A bi-weekly human cleaner at a hundred fifty per visit — three hundred a month. Total first year: around forty-six hundred dollars. And here's the thing — this stack works TODAY. The vacuum doesn't fall over. The cleaner cleans bathrooms, which NEO cannot do. `[AFFILIATE: robot vacuum link goes here — this is the video's conversion point]`

**Option two: NEO on subscription.** Six thousand a year. It covers the tidying and laundry lane — slowly — but you likely still need the vacuum, and you still need the human for deep cleaning. So realistically it's six thousand ON TOP of some of option one, not instead of it.

**Option three: NEO purchased outright.** Twenty thousand up front on a version-one product whose successor — by the company's own roadmap and everyone else's — will be dramatically better and meaningfully cheaper within two to three years. Twenty grand on gen one is what early adopters paid for the first plasma TVs. You know how that went.

So on pure dollars, the boring stack destroys the robot. It's not close. Anyone who tells you NEO "pays for itself" in saved time is doing influencer math.

BUT — and this is where I'll actually defend the robot — pure dollars is the wrong frame for two groups of people. If you have mobility limitations, the calculation isn't "cheaper than a cleaner," it's "can I stay independent in my own home," and against assisted-living costs — sixty thousand a year and up `[VERIFY]` — six grand is nothing. And if you're a developer or a business building on robotics, this is the cheapest general-purpose humanoid platform with real support you can put in a real environment `[VERIFY: vs Unitree G1 pricing ~$16K]`.

For everyone else? The math says wait. And "wait" has its own number, so let's put a number on it.

That's not speculation — that's what happens to every robotics category the moment Chinese supply chains industrialize it. It happened to drones. It happened to robot vacuums, which cost a thousand dollars in 2015 and two hundred now.

So run the wait scenario. You wait until 2028. You've spent zero. The robot you can buy then is a generation better — trained, remember, on the data from today's buyers — and costs, conservatively, thirty percent less. Meanwhile the buyer who subscribed in 2026 spent roughly fourteen thousand dollars in subscription fees to get there, and owns nothing.

The counter-argument — the strongest case FOR buying now — is honestly not about chores at all. It's that you want to be early. Some people bought an Apple II in 1978 and it changed the direction of their whole life, not because it was useful — it barely was — but because they got three years of fluency before everyone else. If twenty thousand dollars is money you can genuinely afford to burn on being three years early to the defining technology of the next decade — that's a real reason. It's just not the reason in the ad.

For the other ninety-five percent of us: the robot you actually want is the one this robot's data is currently training.

### SECTION 5 — THE VERDICT (17:30 – 20:30)

**[GRAPHIC: decision tree — build as narrated]**

So here's the whole video in four sentences.

If you're buying NEO to save money on chores — don't. The boring stack of a robot vacuum plus occasional human help costs less, works better, and works today.

If you have accessibility needs, or you're building on robotics professionally — the math genuinely changes, and NEO is worth a serious look. For accessibility in particular, the subscription structure is actually an advantage: you're renting the capability while it improves, with none of the version-one depreciation risk.

If you want it because it's the future and you can afford to be early — subscribe, don't buy. Four ninety-nine a month with the ability to cancel `[VERIFY: cancellation terms]` is a reasonable price for a front-row seat. Twenty thousand locked into gen-one hardware is not.

And if you're everyone else: wait. Set a mental trigger — when a humanoid can clean a bathroom unattended and costs under ten grand, that's the buy signal. My honest guess is that's 2028, 2029. The best thing you can do until then costs nothing: let other people's subscription fees train your future robot.

What would change my mind? Two things, and I'll cover both on this channel when they happen: full-task autonomy without teleoperation, and a price war once Chinese home humanoids clear US safety certification. Either of those flips this verdict — and one of them is closer than you think. That's the next video.
